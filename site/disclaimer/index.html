5. Risks of Medical Treatment

All medical and surgical procedures carry inherent risks, including but not limited to:

  • Infection and post-surgical complications
  • Adverse reactions to anaesthesia or medications
  • Blood loss and need for transfusion
  • Nerve or organ damage
  • Implant failure or rejection
  • Need for additional or revision surgeries
  • Extended hospital stay beyond initial estimates
  • In rare cases, permanent disability or death

By choosing to undergo medical treatment abroad, you accept these inherent risks. We urge you to discuss all potential risks and complications thoroughly with your treating physician before providing consent.

6. Risks of Medical Travel

Travelling abroad for medical treatment introduces additional considerations:

  • Travel after surgery: Long-haul flights after certain procedures may increase the risk of deep vein thrombosis (DVT) or other complications. Follow your doctor's advice on when it is safe to fly.
  • Follow-up care: Continuity of care may be more challenging when returning to your home country. Ensure you have a clear follow-up plan with both your treating doctor abroad and a local physician at home.
  • Legal recourse: Medical malpractice laws differ across jurisdictions. Legal recourse for medical complications may be governed by the laws of the country where treatment was performed.
  • Language and cultural differences: Despite the availability of interpreters, nuances in communication may arise. Ensure you fully understand all medical information before consenting to treatment.
